DrawnCurve

Design Systems

Three coherent design languages. Each has a distinct shape vocabulary, interaction model for controls, and colour semantics — not the same layout in three colour palettes.

System A

Atlas — warm light, physical weight

Warm-white surfaces. Controls feel placed, not stamped. The direction switch is a thumb that slides — you move it, it snaps. Indigo accent carries selection and motion. Radius is generous; nothing has sharp corners.

System B

Linen — cool paper, forest green

A cooler off-white, closer to drafting paper than ivory. Forest green accent is quieter than blue — supports the curve without competing. Direction switch as three circles on a ledger: discrete, deliberate, no sliding ambiguity. Radius is tighter throughout.

Scale lattice (2A) — Note mode

C major (F lydian) with C♯ + B♭ also active. Filled = in scale · tinted outline = chromatic in scale · ghost outline = excluded. Root gets the outer ring. Natural notes larger, chromatic smaller — no piano skeuomorphism.

System C

Field — cool grey, precision first

Cool blue-grey surfaces. Everything is a bit tighter — less radius, less padding — so more information fits and the curve dominates. Blue accent is clear and unsentimental. The direction control is a three-zone segment: you read what's active in one glance. Monospace values.

Observations

Direction switch — three different interaction models

Atlas (sliding thumb): position encodes state spatially — you can read it without parsing text. Spring snap gives physical feedback. Slightly ambiguous between positions if dragged; good for live performance where you overshoot.

Linen (dots on ledger): three distinct circles with no spatial coupling — you tap a target, not a region. Less discoverable (doesn't look like a selector until you know), but very precise and never accidentally changed. Feels more like a score marking than a control.

Field (segmented zone): clearest at a glance, closest to what's already in the plugin. The active zone is filled, the others ghosted. Zero ambiguity. Risk: looks like every other segmented control.

Scale lattice — from your 04 sketch

The core idea from 04_2A works: natural notes on the bottom row (larger circles), chromatic above (smaller, staggered). The key improvement is contrast — your original had two very similar dark fills for active vs inactive. Here: solid fill = in scale, ghost outline = excluded, tinted fill + coloured border = chromatic-but-active. Root gets an outer ring for orientation. No piano keyboard metaphor anywhere.

On light mode and contrast

All three systems here are light. The background and surface colours are intentionally differentiated (not just one grey), so you can read panel hierarchy at a glance. The curve gets full contrast against the inset surface — it doesn't compete with the controls, which live on slightly raised surfaces. The accent colour does one job: it marks what's active.
